I am Ruoxuan Li (李若瑄), a researcher working at the intersection of cognitive science, data science, and human centered-AI. I am particularly interested in how long-term interactions with large language models (LLMs) and other personalized AI systems shape the way people think about themselves, evaluate their decisions, and decide when to change their behavior.

I completed my B.S. in Cognitive Science (with a minor in Computer Science) at UC San Diego, where I specialized in machine learning and neural networks. I then earned an M.S. in Data Science at Columbia University. Since then, I have worked as a research assistant on multiple research teams at Columbia, collaborating with faculty in psychiatry, psychology, and economics. My research sits between cognitive science, NLP, and HCI. In the long term, I hope to build personalized AI systems that can support people’s metacognition, especially for individuals with diverse cognitive profiles.

Outside of research, I recharge by being outdoors with a camera in hand. I have been birding since middle school and still feel a quiet kind of happiness whenever I spot a new species through the viewfinder. I also enjoy hiking, wandering in cities, and traveling to places that combine good food with interesting conversations.

I always look forward to meeting people who share similar interests or whose work overlaps with mine! Feel free to reach out to me via email!

  1. SIGCSE
    CS0 vs. CS1: Understanding Fears and Confidence amongst Non-majors in Introductory CS Courses
    Ruoxuan* Li, Emma* Hogan, and Adalbert Gerald Soosai Raj
    SIGCSE 2023, Mar 2023
    Abs PDF

    Previous research has been devoted to improving the experience of non-majors in introductory CS courses. In this study, we compare the experiences of non-majors in two different introductory CS courses, specifically with respect to fears about taking the course and change in confidence levels. CS0 is a computing course intentionally designed for non-majors, and CS1 is a more traditional introductory computing course. Both of these courses were composed primarily of non-majors and were taught by the same instructor. Survey data was collected from 124 students enrolled in CS0, and 502 students enrolled in CS1. Through qualitative analysis, we found that the fears of non-major students entering both of these introductory CS courses fell into one or more of nine distinct categories (e.g., Coding, Perceiving STEM as Difficult, Managing Workload). Additionally, using students’ confidence levels at the beginning and end of the courses, we found that students in CS0 had a greater increase in confidence level than those in CS1. Finally, we explored connections between students’ fears and how their confidence changed by the end of the course. We found that students across both courses with fears related to coding, lack of preparation, and being left behind had the highest average increase in confidence levels.
